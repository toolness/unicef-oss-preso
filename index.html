Best practices

  • Keep a changelog to help stakeholders and community members keep up with your project without having to read every commit.
  • Semantic versioning is a low-bandwidth way of quickly communicating to stakeholders (both humans and API clients) what has changed in your project.
  • Open a Pull Request as early as possible - "Pull Requests are a great way to start a conversation of a feature, so start one as soon as possible-even before you are finished with the code. Your team can comment on the feature as it evolves, instead of providing all their feedback at the very end." Aside from being a great development practice, it can also be a great way to work in the open and co-build with your community.
  • Avoid jargon - Going beyond necessary technical terms to write in jargon can alienate potential contributors; especially those with who may have valuable expertise to contribute to your project, but who may be unfamiliar with some aspect of it.
  • Continuous integration - Services like Travis CI and Circle CI are free for open-source projects, and can be a great way to reduce friction for new contributors.
  • Write a great README - CBoard's README is a great example.
  • Establish a code of conduct to foster healthy and constructive community behavior.
